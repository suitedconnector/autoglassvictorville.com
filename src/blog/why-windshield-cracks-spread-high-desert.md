      <h2>The High Desert Is Hard on Windshields</h2>
      <p>If you live in Victorville, Hesperia, or Apple Valley, you already know our weather is extreme. Summer days over 100°F, winter nights below freezing, and daily temperature swings of 40+ degrees. That's rough on your windshield — and it's why small chips here turn into long cracks faster than almost anywhere else.</p>

      <h2>Why Temperature Swings Crack Glass</h2>
      <p>Glass expands when it heats up and contracts when it cools. A healthy windshield handles this fine. But once there's a chip or small crack, every temperature cycle puts stress on that weak point:</p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Morning:</strong> You blast the defroster on a cold windshield. The inside surface heats rapidly while the outside stays cold. The glass layers expand at different rates — stress concentrates at the chip.</li>
        <li><strong>Midday:</strong> Desert sun heats the glass to 130°F+. The crack expands.</li>
        <li><strong>Evening:</strong> Temperatures plummet. The glass contracts. More stress.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>Repeat that cycle daily and a dime-sized chip can become a foot-long crack in a week.</p>

      <h2>Other High Desert Crack Accelerators</h2>

      <h3>Highway Debris</h3>
      <p>The I-15, Highway 395, and Highway 18 see heavy truck traffic. Gravel and debris kicked up at 70 mph cause the initial chips — and hitting a pothole or rough road with an existing chip can extend it instantly.</p>

      <h3>Dust and Dirt</h3>
      <p>Desert dust works its way into open chips. Once dirt contaminates the damage, repairs become less effective and less invisible. A fresh, clean chip repairs far better than one that's been open for weeks.</p>

      <h3>Washboard and Dirt Roads</h3>
      <p>Vibration from unpaved roads around Adelanto, Oak Hills, and Phelan flexes the glass repeatedly, encouraging cracks to travel.</p>

      <h2>How to Slow a Crack Until You Can Get It Fixed</h2>
      <p>These are temporary measures only — but they can buy you a few days:</p>
      <ol>
        <li><strong>Park in shade or a garage.</strong> Avoid direct desert sun on the glass.</li>
        <li><strong>Go easy on the defroster and AC.</strong> Warm or cool the cabin gradually instead of blasting the windshield with extreme temperatures.</li>
        <li><strong>Cover the chip with clear tape.</strong> Keeps dirt and moisture out, which improves repair quality later. Don't cover the driver's view.</li>
        <li><strong>Avoid rough roads and slamming doors.</strong> Pressure spikes and vibration spread cracks.</li>
      </ol>
      <p><strong>What NOT to do:</strong> Skip the DIY resin kits. Poorly applied resin can contaminate the break and make a professional repair impossible — turning a $100 fix into a $400 replacement.</p>

      <h2>The Repair Window Is Short</h2>
      <p>The rule with windshield chips: <strong>repair beats replacement, and sooner beats later.</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>A fresh chip under 1 inch: nearly always repairable</li>
        <li>A crack under 12 inches: usually repairable</li>
        <li>A crack that's spread across the windshield: replacement only</li>
      </ul>
      <p>In High Desert conditions, the window between "repairable chip" and "full replacement" can be days, not weeks.</p>
